Capital Costs and Front-Loaded Investment

Geothermal projects require massive upfront capital expenditures before any revenue generation. Unlike solar and wind, which have seen dramatic cost declines, geothermal remains capital-intensive:

Exploration Risk

Unlike other renewables, geothermal faces high geological uncertainty until confirmation drilling is completed:

  • Drilling costs: 30-57% of overnight capital costs for conventional systems; 50-70% for Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS)
  • Pre-drilling uncertainty: Resource potential unknown until $2-10 million confirmation wells are drilled
  • Timeline risk: Exploratory phases typically take 2+ years before investor confidence
Key Finding: "Most private investors unwilling to accept exploration-phase risk; projects struggle to secure commercial financing before resource confirmation." ‚Äî World Bank/ESMAP (2020)

üèõÔ∏è Political Impediments

Regulatory Fragmentation

Geothermal projects must navigate overlapping federal, state, and local regulations, creating extended timelines and uncertainty:

NREL Finding: Each final geothermal well on U.S. public land invokes NEPA review up to 6 times, with each Environmental Assessment taking approximately 10 months, resulting in an average development timeline of 8 years.

The GeoVision Barriers Task Force Report projects that streamlining permitting could decrease development timelines by 25-50% and increase geothermal capacity by 15-20 times by 2050.

Subsidy Imbalances

Historical and ongoing subsidies favor fossil fuels. In 2016, fossil fuels received approximately $260 billion in global subsidies. Geothermal only gained major federal support with the 2022 Inflation Reduction Act.

International Success Stories

Kenya, Iceland, Philippines

These countries demonstrate that success requires:

  • Consolidated governance structures
  • Clear political commitment at the highest levels
  • Comprehensive policy frameworks
  • Long-term vision beyond electoral cycles

‚öñÔ∏è Legal Impediments

Property Rights Uncertainty

The fundamental legal question of "who owns geothermal heat" remains unresolved in many jurisdictions. Unlike oil and gas with established property rights frameworks, geothermal's classification varies by state:

Jurisdiction Classification Implications
Texas (2023) Surface rights holder owns subsurface geothermal Model legislation resolving decades of uncertainty
Utah Associated with nontributary groundwater Tied to surface rights unless severed
Federal Lands Distinct from minerals (Geothermal Steam Act 1970) Separate regulatory framework
Some states Treated as minerals Requires severed mineral rights
Eckstein (2024): "Unclear property rights in geothermal heat significantly inhibit investment... a problem that persists nearly 50 years" since first documented. ‚Äî Illinois Law Review

Induced Seismicity Liability

Legal frameworks for induced seismicity liability remain unclear, creating significant investment risk:

  • Basel, Switzerland (2006): EGS project terminated after ML 3.4 earthquake
  • Pohang, Korea (2017): Mw 5.4 earthquake caused $52M in damage
  • Strasbourg, France: Project terminated due to ML 3.6 induced event

Recent Reform Efforts

BLM proposed categorical exclusions in January 2025 to streamline permitting. Research shows LCOE is 4-11% higher for projects with longer environmental review timelines.

üßë‚Äçü§ù‚Äçüßë Social Impediments

Key Barriers

Social barriers often equal or exceed technical challenges:

  • Lack of awareness: Most fundamental barrier affecting all other acceptance factors
  • Seismic risk fears: Particularly for Enhanced Geothermal Systems
  • NIMBY opposition: Support in principle, oppose in locality
  • Trust deficits: Especially between developers and local communities

Case Studies

üáØüáµ Japan: Hot Springs Conflict

Kusatsu, Unzen, and other onsen towns

Hot spring (onsen) resort owners have blocked geothermal development for decades, fearing depletion of thermal water resources‚Äîdespite different drilling depths. Town of Kusatsu passed ordinance requiring proof that projects won't negatively affect hot springs.

Success story: Obama Hot Springs in Unzen developed community consensus through extensive outreach and local councils with spa owners as voting members.

üá∞üá∑ Korea: Pohang Earthquake

Pohang, South Korea (2017)

Mw 5.4 earthquake near Enhanced Geothermal System project caused $52 million in damage and injured 90 people. Proximity to epicenter negatively correlated with public preference for geothermal energy. Local residents began anchoring geothermal risk to nuclear power plant risks.

üá∞üá™ Kenya: Maasai Displacement

Olkaria Geothermal Area

Over 1,000 Maasai people involuntarily relocated in 2014. Research documented exacerbation of inequality along lines of gender, age, and ethnicity. Revealed processes of resource capture and exclusionary decision-making.

üá≥üáø New Zealand: MƒÅori Co-Management

Various sites

Success model: Indigenous partnership models demonstrate success when rights are respected and benefits shared equitably. Provides template for community engagement.

Methods Used in the Literature

  • Semi-structured interviews ‚Äî Most common (15-50 participants typical)
  • Focus group discussions ‚Äî 4-8 groups, often gender-segregated
  • Participant observation ‚Äî Rare but powerful (1-7 months fieldwork)
  • Case study designs ‚Äî Single and comparative
  • Deliberative methods ‚Äî Citizen juries, participatory action research
  • Document/media analysis ‚Äî EIA reports, newspapers, policy documents

Research Gaps Identified

üåç Geographic Gaps

Limited research in Africa (outside East Africa), Middle East, Central Asia, much of Latin America

üìã Methodological Gaps

Few longitudinal ethnographies; limited indigenous-led research; underuse of visual/sensory methods

üìö Topical Gaps

Weak on intergenerational issues, class/economic diversity, disability, emotional dimensions, success stories

üéì Theoretical Gaps

Limited critical theory integration; weak power/political economy analysis

Opportunity for Future Research

The literature reveals significant opportunities for deeper ethnographic engagement‚Äîparticularly longitudinal studies, indigenous-led research, and work that integrates power analysis and political economy perspectives. Most existing research relies on interviews and surveys; immersive participant observation remains rare.
