üí∞ Economic Impediments

Capital Costs and Front-Loaded Investment

Geothermal projects require massive upfront capital expenditures before any revenue generation. Capital costs range from $3,000-$7,000/kW [1], significantly higher than solar ($925-$1,250/kW) and wind ($1,250-$1,750/kW) [1]. Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS) face even higher costs at $5,000-$6,000/kW [2].

Source: Center for Sustainable Systems, University of Michigan. (2024). "Geothermal Energy Factsheet." css.umich.edu

Exploration Risk

Drilling costs represent 30-57% of overnight capital costs for conventional systems and 50-70% for EGS projects [3]. Pre-drilling resource potential remains unknown until confirmation wells costing $2-10 million each are drilled, with no guarantee of success [4].

Key Finding: "Most private investors unwilling to accept exploration-phase risk; projects struggle to secure commercial financing before resource confirmation."
Source: World Bank/ESMAP. (2020). "The Global Geothermal Development Plan." worldbank.org

üèõÔ∏è Political Impediments

Regulatory Fragmentation

Each final geothermal well on U.S. public land invokes NEPA review up to 6 times, with each Environmental Assessment taking approximately 10 months, resulting in an average development timeline of 8 years [5].

GeoVision Analysis: Streamlining permitting could decrease development timelines by 25-50% and increase geothermal capacity by 15-20 times by 2050.
Source: Young, K. R., et al. (2019). GeoVision Analysis Supporting Task Force Report: Barriers. NREL/TP-6A20-71641. nrel.gov

Fossil Fuel Lobbying Asymmetry

In 2022, the oil and gas sector invested approximately $124.4 million on federal lobbying efforts, far exceeding renewable energy industry spending [6].

Subsidy Imbalances

In 2016, fossil fuels received approximately $260 billion in global subsidies [7]. Geothermal only gained major U.S. federal support with the 2022 Inflation Reduction Act [8].

‚öñÔ∏è Legal Impediments

Property Rights Uncertainty

The legal ownership of geothermal resources remains contested and varies by jurisdiction [9]. Recent Texas legislation (effective mid-2023) declared that surface rights holders own subsurface geothermal energy, providing a model for resolving decades of uncertainty [10].

Jurisdiction Classification Source
Texas (2023) Surface rights holder owns subsurface geothermal [SPE 2023]
Utah Associated with nontributary groundwater [Utah Legislature]
Federal Lands Distinct from minerals (Geothermal Steam Act 1970) [30 U.S.C. ¬ß1001]
Eckstein (2024): "Unclear property rights in geothermal heat significantly inhibit investment... a problem that persists nearly 50 years" since first documented.
Source: Eckstein, G. (2024). "Who Owns Heat? Property Rights in Geothermal Energy." Illinois Law Review. SSRN

Induced Seismicity Liability

Legal frameworks for induced seismicity remain unclear, creating investment risk:

Pohang, South Korea (2017)

Mw 5.4 earthquake near Enhanced Geothermal System project caused $52 million in damage and injured 90 people.

Source: Ellsworth, W.L., et al. (2019). "Triggering of the Pohang, Korea, Earthquake by Enhanced Geothermal System Stimulation." Science. science.org

Permitting Cost Impact

Analysis found LCOE was 4-11% higher for projects with longer CEQA/NEPA review timelines compared to projects with the fastest review timelines [11].

üßë‚Äçü§ù‚Äçüßë Social Impediments

Key Barriers

Social barriers often equal or exceed technical challenges. A systematic review found that integration of all relevant stakeholder groups strongly influences social acceptance outcomes [12]. Common barriers include:

  • Lack of awareness: Most fundamental barrier affecting all other acceptance factors [13]
  • Seismic risk fears: Particularly for Enhanced Geothermal Systems [14]
  • NIMBY opposition: Support in principle, oppose in locality [15]

Case Studies

üáØüáµ Japan: Hot Springs Conflict

Kusatsu, Unzen, and other onsen towns

Hot spring (onsen) resort owners have blocked geothermal development for decades, fearing depletion of thermal water resources‚Äîdespite different drilling depths. Town of Kusatsu passed ordinance requiring proof that projects won't negatively affect hot springs.

Sources: Kubota, H. & Hienuki, S. (2023). "Hot Spring Stakeholder Opposition in Japan." NY Times; Tokyo Foundation for Policy Research. tokyofoundation.org

üá∞üá™ Kenya: Maasai Displacement

Olkaria Geothermal Area

Over 1,000 Maasai people involuntarily relocated in 2014. Research documented exacerbation of inequality along lines of gender, age, and ethnicity, revealing processes of resource capture and exclusionary decision-making.

Source: Ricker, F.N. (2022). "Geothermal Development in Kenya: Gender Based Socio-Cultural and Socio-Economic Implications." Ph.D. Dissertation, University of South Florida. USF Digital Commons

üá≥üáø New Zealand: MƒÅori Co-Management

Various sites

Success model: Indigenous partnership models demonstrate success when rights are respected and benefits shared equitably.

Source: Barasa, M., et al. (2024). "Social acceptance of geothermal energy." Clean Energy, 8(5). Oxford Academic

üåç Geographic Gaps

Limited research in Africa (outside East Africa), Middle East, Central Asia, much of Latin America

üìã Methodological Gaps

Few longitudinal ethnographies; limited indigenous-led research; underuse of visual/sensory methods

üìö Topical Gaps

Weak on intergenerational issues, class/economic diversity, emotional dimensions

üéì Theoretical Gaps

Limited critical theory integration; weak power/political economy analysis
